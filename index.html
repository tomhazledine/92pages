Our Process

Back to top

What 92pages will not do is take a brief and then disappear for months, only returning for a Big Reveal™ at the end of the project. We take an agile approach to managing projects; dealing with features in short sprints, testing and reviewing, and then refining. Test, Measure, Learn.

To be sure of delivering a successful end-product, we need to break the site down into sections. These sections should be deliverable features that can be built quickly and tested early.

A typical project may include the following example sections:

  • Initial Sprint: The minimum viable product (MVP)

    The MVP represents the smallest amount of work we can do to get something in front of test users. Typically this is a content-based website that conforms to the brand guidelines and conveys the appropriate tone of voice.

  • Feature Sprints

    Feature Sprints tackle specific areas of functionality. Need a blog, for instance? That's a Feature Sprint.

    We'd then build the necessary functionality (in the case of a blog: the ability for a site owner to log-in and create/manage/delete content, and for a site user to access/browse/search that content). The sprint ends when that feature ships, which means we can get it in front of testers and a small cohort of real users.

The power of a sprint-based approach is twofold:

  • The client sees regular progress.

    You can be a regular collaborator in the project. This ensures we stay on track, and that you get the site you really want.

  • Testing happens sooner.

    User Experience issues are identified sooner, and can therefore be addressed more efficiently.

    All devices can be tested from the beginning.

    Accessibility concerns are addressed throughout the process, not as an afterthought.

Sometimes needs change, or ideas — once tested — just don't work. Sometimes alternative options present themselves out of the ether… Regular reviewing of the project's objectives and progress keeps things moving in the right direction.

This allows us to get a working product in the hands of testers as early as possible. Catching issues early helps us stay on-budget and on-time. If we catch issues early, addressing them is much faster.

CMS & Third Party

Back to top

It would be our recommendation to use WordPress for the Content Management System (CMS). WordPress is an industry leading open source CMS that provides the flexibility and speed required for the new Girl Guiding Scotland website.

WordPress is an open source CMS and there is no annual license fee, it’s easy to learn for content editors and you’re not tired to one development agency for future updates.

In the past WordPress has been seen as a blogging platform, and it still is. However it’s evolved over the last couple of years to become one of the most powerful, versatile and used Content Management Systems available. It’s large following means there are plenty of plugins out there that can be used to easily add functionality to a website. These include, event calendars, booking modules, galleries and even a social network.

In your requirements you mention the different levels of access, this something that can be setup using the different roles within the admin area. An additional free plugin can be used to define extra roles such as only allowing the Chief Executive to see the job adverts.

301 redirects are important not only to ensure the user is shown the right page they may have previously bookmarked but also from the Search Engine perspective to keep preserve and previous rankings. We can provide the facility to ensure these are mapped over. This process works by looking at the top indexed pages in Google and working with you to help identify the new alternative page. This is a process that is done once all the new content has been added.

At this point it’s hard to know what third party plugins will be required, however licensing needs to be considered for any fonts that are used. Subscription based services like fonts.com are available for a fee of around £120 per year. You will be notified during the design presentation of any costs that are involved around fonts and images. If any stock images need to be purchased this will be at an additional cost.
